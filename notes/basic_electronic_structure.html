Basics of Electronic Structure

We start from the full molecular Hamiltonian, apply the Born–Oppenheimer approximation, discuss antisymmetry and spin, build Hartree products and Slater determinants, and then derive the ideas behind Hartree–Fock and Configuration Interaction (CI).

1. Introduction

We need to solve the time–independent Schrödinger equation

\[ \hat{H}\Psi = E \Psi. \tag{1} \]

with the full molecular Hamiltonian

\[ \hat{H} = -\sum_{i}^{N}\frac{1}{2}\nabla_i^2 -\sum_{A}^{M}\frac{1}{2M_A}\nabla_A^2 -\sum_{i,A}\frac{Z_A}{|\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{R}_A|} +\sum_{i < j} \frac{1}{|\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{r}_j|} +\sum_{A < B} \frac{Z_A Z_B}{|\mathbf{R}_A-\mathbf{R}_B|}. \tag{2} \]

Here, \(i,j\) label electrons and \(A,B\) label nuclei. The terms represent electronic kinetic energy, nuclear kinetic energy, electron–nuclear attraction, electron–electron repulsion, and nuclear–nuclear repulsion respectively.

We reduce the complexity of Eq. (2) using the Born–Oppenheimer (BO) approximation, which is basically ignoring the nuclear kinetic energy due to the very high \(M_A/m_e\) ratio.

\[ \hat{H}_e \Phi_e = E_e \Phi_e, \tag{5} \]

where \(E_e = E_e(\{\mathbf{R}_A\})\) and \(\Phi_e = \Phi_e(\{\mathbf{r}_i\};\{\mathbf{R}_A\})\) now have parametric dependence on the nuclear coordinates.

The total energy for fixed nuclei is

\[ E_{\text{tot}} = E_e + E_{nn}. \tag{6} \]

2. Antisymmetry Principle

Electrons need both spatial and spin description. So we introduce two spin functions \(\alpha(\omega)\) and \(\beta(\omega)\) such that

\[ \langle \alpha | \beta \rangle = 0. \tag{7} \]

The electron is thus described by two types of coordinates, collectively,

\[ \mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{r},\omega). \tag{8} \]

Since spin is a fundamental property determining electron statistics, we must incorporate the spin–statistics of electrons in the electronic wavefunction through the antisymmetry principle,

\[ \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_i,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_j,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_N) = - \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_j,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_i,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_N). \tag{9} \]

3. Orbitals, Hartree Products, and Slater Determinants

3.1 Orbitals and spin orbitals

An orbital is a wavefunction for a single particle. A spatial orbital is a function of the position vector of a particle, but an electron also needs a spin description. Hence an orbital wavefunction of a single electron must contain spin as well.

We refer to such an orbital as a spin orbital. From each spatial orbital \(\phi_i(\mathbf{r})\) we can form two spin orbitals as

\[ \chi_{i\alpha}(\mathbf{x}) = \phi_i(\mathbf{r})\alpha(\omega),\qquad \chi_{i\beta}(\mathbf{x}) = \phi_i(\mathbf{r})\beta(\omega). \tag{10} \]

3.2 Hartree products

Let us consider a simple system of non–interacting electrons. The Hamiltonian can be written as

\[ \hat{H} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \hat{h}(i). \tag{11} \]

Since \(\hat{H}\) is just a sum of independent one–electron Hamiltonians, we can use separation of variables to suggest the eigenfunctions as

\[ \Psi_H(\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_N) = \chi_{i}(\mathbf{x}_1)\chi_{j}(\mathbf{x}_2)\cdots\chi_{n}(\mathbf{x}_N). \tag{12} \]

Equation (12) is known as a Hartree product. This is an uncorrelated (independent electron) wavefunction since the probability density factorizes.

3.3 Slater determinants

The simple Hartree product will not qualify for a multi–electronic system, even if we ignore all electron–electron repulsions. To apply antisymmetry, let us first look at a two–electron system.

\[ \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1,\mathbf{x}_2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left[ \chi_i(\mathbf{x}_1)\chi_j(\mathbf{x}_2) - \chi_i(\mathbf{x}_2)\chi_j(\mathbf{x}_1) \right]. \tag{13} \]

The generalization to \(N\) electrons is

\[ \Psi(\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_N) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N!}} \begin{vmatrix} \chi_1(\mathbf{x}_1) & \chi_2(\mathbf{x}_1) & \cdots & \chi_N(\mathbf{x}_1) \\ \chi_1(\mathbf{x}_2) & \chi_2(\mathbf{x}_2) & \cdots & \chi_N(\mathbf{x}_2) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \chi_1(\mathbf{x}_N) & \chi_2(\mathbf{x}_N) & \cdots & \chi_N(\mathbf{x}_N) \end{vmatrix}. \tag{14} \]

4. Exchange Effect and Hartree–Fock Approximation

Within a single Slater determinant, the motion of electrons with parallel spins is correlated (purely due to antisymmetry). This is the so–called exchange correlation, and the “Fermi hole” picture can be understood from the fact that for two same–spin electrons, the joint probability vanishes at coincident points.

Our simplest guess for the many–electron wavefunction is to use a single Slater determinant of \(N\) non–interacting electrons,

\[ \Phi_{\mathrm{HF}} = |\chi_1 \chi_2 \cdots \chi_N|. \tag{15} \]

Using the variational principle,

\[ E_{\mathrm{HF}} = \frac{\langle \Phi_{\mathrm{HF}} | \hat{H}_e | \Phi_{\mathrm{HF}} \rangle} {\langle \Phi_{\mathrm{HF}} | \Phi_{\mathrm{HF}} \rangle} \ge E_{\mathrm{ground}}. \tag{16} \]

The Hartree–Fock equation is an eigenvalue equation for spin orbitals,

\[ \hat{f}(i)\chi_i(\mathbf{x}) = \varepsilon_i \chi_i(\mathbf{x}), \tag{17} \]

where \(\hat{f}\) is the Fock operator.

4.1 Self–Consistent Field (SCF) procedure

  1. Choose a nuclear geometry \(\{\mathbf{R}_A\}\).
  2. Guess an initial set of \(N\) spin orbitals \(\{\chi_i\}\).
  3. Construct the Fock operator using the current orbitals.
  4. Solve the Hartree–Fock equations to obtain new orbitals.
  5. Compare the new orbitals with the previous ones.
  6. Repeat until self–consistency is achieved.

5. Configuration Interaction (CI)

Once we obtain the virtual orbitals from the Hartree–Fock procedure, we can use them to construct excited Slater determinants and then build a CI expansion.

\[ |\Psi\rangle = c_0 |\Phi_0\rangle + \sum_{ai} c_i^a |\Phi_i^a\rangle + \sum_{abij} c_{ij}^{ab} |\Phi_{ij}^{ab}\rangle + \cdots. \tag{18} \]

The energy difference between the exact CI solution and the Hartree–Fock energy is called the correlation energy,

\[ E_{\mathrm{corr}} = E_{\mathrm{exact}} - E_{\mathrm{HF}}. \tag{19} \]

6. Operator Matrix Elements Between Slater Determinants

To understand Hartree–Fock internally, we need the operator algebra between Slater determinants. The electronic Hamiltonian can be written as

\[ \hat{H}_e = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \hat{h}(i) + \sum_{i < j}^{N} \hat{g}(i,j), \tag{20} \]

where

\[ \hat{h}(i) = -\frac{1}{2}\nabla_i^2 -\sum_A \frac{Z_A}{|\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{R}_A|}, \qquad \hat{g}(i,j) = \frac{1}{|\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{r}_j|}. \tag{21} \]

We define the one–electron integral

\[ h_{ij} = \langle \chi_i | \hat{h} | \chi_j \rangle = \int d\mathbf{x}\, \chi_i^*(\mathbf{x}) \hat{h} \chi_j(\mathbf{x}). \tag{22} \]

and the two–electron integral

\[ (ij|kl) = \iint d\mathbf{x}_1 d\mathbf{x}_2\, \chi_i^*(1)\chi_j^*(2) \frac{1}{r_{12}} \chi_k(1)\chi_l(2). \tag{23} \]

The Coulomb integral \((ij|ij)\) has a classical meaning; the exchange integral \((ij|ji)\) arises purely from quantum exchange. Using these, the Hartree–Fock energy for a single Slater determinant becomes

\[ E_{\mathrm{HF}} = \sum_m h_{mm} + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{m,n}\left[(mn|mn) - (mn|nm)\right]. \tag{24} \]
