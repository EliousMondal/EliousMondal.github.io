A Fully Detailed, Beginner-Friendly Derivation of the Chebyshev Propagator

This page is a faithful HTML + MathJax conversion of the complete PDF notes (all sections, all equations, all steps, and both 2×2 examples).

1. When is Chebyshev propagator needed?

1.1 Time evolution as an operator exponential

In quantum mechanics (setting \(\hbar = 1\)), a time-independent Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}\) generates dynamics via the Schrödinger equation

\[ i\,\frac{d}{dt}\,|\psi(t)\rangle = \hat{H}\,|\psi(t)\rangle. \]
(1)

The formal solution is

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = e^{-i\hat{H}t}\,|\psi(0)\rangle. \]
(2)

Thus, the central computational task is:

Task: Given \(\hat{H}\) and \(|\psi(0)\rangle\), compute \(|\psi(t)\rangle = e^{-i\hat{H}t}|\psi(0)\rangle\).

1.2 Why we do not want to diagonalize

If the Hilbert space dimension is large, diagonalizing \(\hat{H}\) or forming the dense matrix \(e^{-i\hat{H}t}\) is impractical. However, we only need the action of the propagator on a vector:

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = \big(e^{-i\hat{H}t}\big)|\psi(0)\rangle. \]
(3)

Chebyshev propagation achieves this by approximating the operator exponential by a polynomial series in \(\hat{H}\) (after rescaling), and evaluating the series using a stable three-term recurrence that requires only repeated matrix–vector multiplications.

1.3 High-level plan

We will proceed in the following logically connected steps:

  1. Define Chebyshev polynomials \(T_n(x)\), prove their boundedness on \([-1,1]\), and derive their recurrence.
  2. Explain why the Hamiltonian must be rescaled so its spectrum lies in \([-1,1]\).
  3. Derive the scalar Chebyshev expansion of \(e^{-i\tau x}\) for \(x\in[-1,1]\): \(e^{-i\tau x}=J_0(\tau)+2\sum_{n\ge1}(-i)^nJ_n(\tau)T_n(x)\).
  4. Lift the scalar identity to an operator identity \(x\mapsto\hat{H}'\) via the eigenbasis of \(\hat{H}'\).
  5. Derive the Chebyshev propagation algorithm (vector recurrence).
  6. Derive a truncation error bound and explain why \(J_n(\tau)\) becomes small for \(n\gtrsim\tau\).
  7. Work through explicit \(2\times2\) examples, including nontrivial rescaling.

2. Chebyshev Polynomials: Definition, Properties, and Recurrence

2.1 Step 1: The cosine parametrization of \([-1,1]\)

A key observation is that any \(x\in[-1,1]\) can be written as

\[ x = \cos\theta,\qquad \theta\in[0,\pi]. \]
(4)

This is simply the fact that \(\cos\theta\) maps \([0,\pi]\) onto \([-1,1]\).

2.2 Step 2: Definition of Chebyshev polynomials

Definition. The Chebyshev polynomial of the first kind is defined by

\[ T_n(x) \equiv \cos(n\theta)\quad\text{where }x=\cos\theta. \]
(5)

This definition immediately implies that \(T_n(x)\) is a polynomial in \(x\) (provable by trigonometric identities).

2.3 Step 3: First few polynomials (explicit)

Using \(\cos(2\theta)=2\cos^2\theta-1\) and \(\cos(3\theta)=4\cos^3\theta-3\cos\theta\), we obtain

\[ T_0(x)=\cos(0)=1. \]
(6)
\[ T_1(x)=\cos\theta=x. \]
(7)
\[ T_2(x)=\cos(2\theta)=2x^2-1. \]
(8)
\[ T_3(x)=\cos(3\theta)=4x^3-3x. \]
(9)

2.4 Step 4: Boundedness on \([-1,1]\)

From the definition (5), for \(x=\cos\theta\in[-1,1]\),

\[ |T_n(x)|=|\cos(n\theta)|\le 1. \]
(10)

This boundedness is crucial for numerical stability: the polynomials do not blow up on \([-1,1]\).

2.5 Step 5: The three-term recurrence (derived)

Start from the cosine identity

\[ \cos\big((n+1)\theta\big)=2\cos\theta\cos(n\theta)-\cos\big((n-1)\theta\big). \]
(11)

Now substitute \(\cos\theta=x\), \(\cos(n\theta)=T_n(x)\), and \(\cos((n-1)\theta)=T_{n-1}(x)\):

\[ T_{n+1}(x)=2x\,T_n(x)-T_{n-1}(x). \]
(12)

This recurrence lets us build high-degree \(T_n(x)\) efficiently and stably.

3. Why We Rescale the Hamiltonian to \([-1,1]\)

3.1 Step 1: Eigenvalues determine operator functions

Let \(\hat{H}\) be Hermitian. Then it has orthonormal eigenstates \(\{|E_k\rangle\}\) with real eigenvalues \(\{E_k\}\):

\[ \hat{H}|E_k\rangle = E_k|E_k\rangle,\qquad E_k\in\mathbb{R}. \]
(13)

The propagator acts on eigenstates as

\[ e^{-i\hat{H}t}|E_k\rangle = e^{-iE_k t}|E_k\rangle. \]
(14)

So approximating \(e^{-i\hat{H}t}\) reduces to approximating the scalar function \(e^{-iEt}\) for all eigenvalues \(E\) in the spectrum.

3.2 Step 2: Why Chebyshev polynomials demand \(x\in[-1,1]\)

Chebyshev polynomials are bounded on \([-1,1]\) by (10). Outside that interval, \(T_n(x)\) grows rapidly with \(n\) (in fact exponentially). Thus, to ensure numerical stability when evaluating \(T_n(\hat{H}')\) at large \(n\), we want the spectrum of the operator to lie inside \([-1,1]\).

3.3 Step 3: Rescaling the spectrum

Assume we have bounds

\[ E_{\min}\le E_k\le E_{\max}\quad\text{for all eigenvalues}. \]
(15)

Define

\[ a=\frac{E_{\max}+E_{\min}}{2},\qquad b=\frac{E_{\max}-E_{\min}}{2}>0, \]
(16)

and the shifted/rescaled Hamiltonian

\[ \hat{H}'=\frac{\hat{H}-a\hat{I}}{b}. \]
(17)

If \(\hat{H}|E_k\rangle=E_k|E_k\rangle\), then

\[ \hat{H}'|E_k\rangle = \frac{E_k-a}{b}|E_k\rangle \equiv E_k'|E_k\rangle. \]
(18)

Now check endpoints:

If \(E_k=E_{\max}\), then \(E_k'=(E_{\max}-a)/b=+1\). If \(E_k=E_{\min}\), then \(E_k'=(E_{\min}-a)/b=-1\). Hence

\[ E_k'\in[-1,1]. \]
(19)

3.4 Step 4: Factorizing the propagator

Rewrite \(\hat{H}=a\hat{I}+b\hat{H}'\). Then

\[ e^{-i\hat{H}t}=e^{-i(a\hat{I}+b\hat{H}')t}. \]
(20)

Because \(\hat{I}\) commutes with \(\hat{H}'\) and \(e^{X+Y}=e^Xe^Y\) when \([X,Y]=0\), we get

\[ e^{-i\hat{H}t}=e^{-iat}\,e^{-i(bt)\hat{H}'}. \]
(21)

Define the scaled time

\[ \tau\equiv bt. \]
(22)

Then the numerical problem becomes computing \(e^{-i\tau\hat{H}'}\) with \(\mathrm{spec}(\hat{H}')\subset[-1,1]\).

4. Scalar Chebyshev Expansion of \(e^{-i\tau x}\) on \([-1,1]\)

We now derive the key scalar identity:

\[ e^{-i\tau x}=J_0(\tau)+2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(-i)^n J_n(\tau)\,T_n(x),\qquad x\in[-1,1]. \]
(23)

4.1 Step 1: Change variables \(x=\cos\theta\)

Let \(x\in[-1,1]\) and write \(x=\cos\theta\) with \(\theta\in[0,\pi]\). Then

\[ e^{-i\tau x}=e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}. \]
(24)

The function \(g(\theta)=e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\) is a smooth function of \(\theta\).

4.2 Step 2: Why the Fourier expansion has only cosines (no sines)

Consider extending \(g(\theta)=e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\) to \([ -\pi,\pi ]\). Because \(\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta\), we have

\[ g(-\theta)=e^{-i\tau\cos(-\theta)}=e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}=g(\theta). \]
(25)

So \(g(\theta)\) is an even function. In Fourier series, even functions contain only cosine terms.

Integral proof (explicit). The sine Fourier coefficient over a symmetric interval is

\[ b_n=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} g(\theta)\,\sin(n\theta)\,d\theta. \]
(26)

Here \(g(\theta)\) is even, \(\sin(n\theta)\) is odd, so their product is odd. The integral of an odd function over \([-\pi,\pi]\) is zero, hence \(b_n=0\) for all \(n\).

Therefore we expand \(g(\theta)\) as a cosine series:

\[ e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}=a_0(\tau)+2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n(\tau)\cos(n\theta). \]
(27)

4.3 Step 3: Compute the cosine coefficients by orthogonality

On \([0,\pi]\), cosine functions satisfy orthogonality:

\[ \int_{0}^{\pi} \cos(n\theta)\cos(m\theta)\,d\theta = \begin{cases} 0, & n\ne m,\\ \dfrac{\pi}{2}, & n=m\ge 1,\\ \pi, & n=m=0. \end{cases} \]
(28)

Multiply both sides of (27) by \(\cos(m\theta)\) and integrate from \(0\) to \(\pi\).

Case \(m=0\). Integrate:

\[ \int_{0}^{\pi} e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\,d\theta = a_0(\tau)\int_{0}^{\pi}1\,d\theta + 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n(\tau)\int_{0}^{\pi}\cos(n\theta)\,d\theta. \]
(29)

But \(\int_{0}^{\pi}\cos(n\theta)\,d\theta=0\) for \(n\ge 1\). Thus

\[ \int_{0}^{\pi} e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\,d\theta = a_0(\tau)\,\pi, \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad a_0(\tau)=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{0}^{\pi} e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\,d\theta. \]
(30)

Case \(m\ge 1\). Multiply (27) by \(\cos(m\theta)\) and integrate:

\[ \int_{0}^{\pi} e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\cos(m\theta)\,d\theta = a_0(\tau)\int_{0}^{\pi}\cos(m\theta)\,d\theta + 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n(\tau)\int_{0}^{\pi}\cos(n\theta)\cos(m\theta)\,d\theta. \]
(31)

The first term vanishes because \(\int_0^{\pi}\cos(m\theta)\,d\theta=0\) for \(m\ge 1\). Using orthogonality (28), only the \(n=m\) term survives:

\[ \int_{0}^{\pi} e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\cos(m\theta)\,d\theta = 2a_m(\tau)\cdot\frac{\pi}{2}=a_m(\tau)\pi. \]
(32)

Hence

\[ a_m(\tau)=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{0}^{\pi} e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\cos(m\theta)\,d\theta,\qquad m\ge 1. \]
(33)

4.4 Step 4: Define Bessel functions using these integrals

At this point we define the Bessel function of the first kind via the standard integral representation:

\[ J_n(\tau)\equiv \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{0}^{\pi}\cos\big(n\theta-\tau\sin\theta\big)\,d\theta. \]
(34)

A closely related (and equally standard) representation implies the identity

\[ \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{0}^{\pi} e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\cos(n\theta)\,d\theta = (-i)^n J_n(\tau). \]
(35)

(You can regard (35) as a known theorem about Bessel functions or as an equivalent definition; the point is that the Fourier coefficients of \(e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\) are proportional to \(J_n(\tau)\).)

Combining (33) with (35) gives

\[ a_n(\tau)=(-i)^n J_n(\tau). \]
(36)

Substitute into (27):

\[ e^{-i\tau\cos\theta} = J_0(\tau)+2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-i)^n J_n(\tau)\cos(n\theta). \]
(37)

4.5 Step 5: Convert cosines to Chebyshev polynomials

Recall \(x=\cos\theta\). By the Chebyshev definition (5),

\[ \cos(n\theta)=T_n(\cos\theta)=T_n(x). \]
(38)

Thus (37) becomes the desired Chebyshev series:

\[ e^{-i\tau x}=J_0(\tau)+2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-i)^n J_n(\tau)T_n(x),\qquad x\in[-1,1]. \]
(39)

5. Lifting the Scalar Identity to an Operator Identity

We now show carefully why replacing \(x\to\hat{H}'\) is valid.

5.1 Step 1: Eigen-decomposition of \(\hat{H}'\)

Because \(\hat{H}'\) is Hermitian, it has eigenpairs \(\{E_k',|E_k\rangle\}\) with \(E_k'\in[-1,1]\):

\[ \hat{H}'|E_k\rangle = E_k'|E_k\rangle. \]
(40)

Completeness:

\[ \hat{I} = \sum_k |E_k\rangle\langle E_k|. \]
(41)

5.2 Step 2: Action of \(e^{-i\tau\hat{H}'}\) on eigenstates

By definition of operator functions for Hermitian operators,

\[ e^{-i\tau\hat{H}'}|E_k\rangle = e^{-i\tau E_k'}|E_k\rangle. \]
(42)

5.3 Step 3: Apply the scalar identity to each eigenvalue

Since \(E_k'\in[-1,1]\), apply (39) with \(x=E_k'\):

\[ e^{-i\tau E_k'} = J_0(\tau)+2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-i)^n J_n(\tau) T_n(E_k'). \]
(43)

Plug into (42):

\[ e^{-i\tau\hat{H}'}|E_k\rangle = \Big[J_0(\tau)+2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-i)^n J_n(\tau) T_n(E_k')\Big]|E_k\rangle. \]
(44)

5.4 Step 4: Define \(T_n(\hat{H}')\) via spectral calculus

Define the operator polynomial \(T_n(\hat{H}')\) as

\[ T_n(\hat{H}')\equiv \sum_k T_n(E_k')|E_k\rangle\langle E_k|. \]
(45)

Then by construction,

\[ T_n(\hat{H}')|E_k\rangle = T_n(E_k')|E_k\rangle. \]
(46)

Hence (44) can be rewritten as

\[ e^{-i\tau\hat{H}'}|E_k\rangle = \Big[ J_0(\tau)\hat{I} + 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-i)^n J_n(\tau) T_n(\hat{H}') \Big]|E_k\rangle. \]
(47)

Because \(|E_k\rangle\) form a basis, two operators that act identically on all basis vectors are equal. Therefore,

\[ e^{-i\tau\hat{H}'} = J_0(\tau)\hat{I} + 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-i)^n J_n(\tau) T_n(\hat{H}'). \]
(48)

Finally, restore the original Hamiltonian using (21) and \(\tau=bt\):

\[ e^{-i\hat{H}t} = e^{-iat}\Big[ J_0(bt)\hat{I} + 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-i)^n J_n(bt) T_n(\hat{H}') \Big]. \]
(49)

6. Chebyshev Propagation Algorithm (Vector Recurrence)

6.1 Step 1: Apply the expansion to \(|\psi(0)\rangle\)

Define

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = e^{-i\hat{H}t}|\psi(0)\rangle. \]
(50)

Using (49),

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = e^{-iat}\Big[ J_0(\tau)|\psi(0)\rangle + 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-i)^n J_n(\tau)\,T_n(\hat{H}')|\psi(0)\rangle \Big], \quad \tau=bt. \]
(51)

6.2 Step 2: Define Chebyshev vectors

Define the Chebyshev vectors

\[ |\phi_n\rangle \equiv T_n(\hat{H}')|\psi(0)\rangle. \]
(52)

Then (51) becomes

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = e^{-iat}\Big[ J_0(\tau)|\phi_0\rangle + 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-i)^n J_n(\tau)|\phi_n\rangle \Big]. \]
(53)

6.3 Step 3: Initialize \(|\phi_0\rangle\), \(|\phi_1\rangle\)

Since \(T_0(x)=1\) and \(T_1(x)=x\),

\[ |\phi_0\rangle = T_0(\hat{H}')|\psi(0)\rangle = \hat{I}|\psi(0)\rangle = |\psi(0)\rangle. \]
(54)
\[ |\phi_1\rangle = T_1(\hat{H}')|\psi(0)\rangle = \hat{H}'|\psi(0)\rangle. \]
(55)

6.4 Step 4: Derive the recurrence for \(|\phi_n\rangle\)

Using the polynomial recurrence (12) with \(x\to\hat{H}'\):

\[ T_{n+1}(\hat{H}') = 2\hat{H}'T_n(\hat{H}') - T_{n-1}(\hat{H}'). \]
(56)

Apply both sides to \(|\psi(0)\rangle\):

\[ |\phi_{n+1}\rangle = T_{n+1}(\hat{H}')|\psi(0)\rangle. \]
(57)
\[ |\phi_{n+1}\rangle = \big(2\hat{H}'T_n(\hat{H}') - T_{n-1}(\hat{H}')\big)|\psi(0)\rangle. \]
(58)
\[ |\phi_{n+1}\rangle = 2\hat{H}'\underbrace{T_n(\hat{H}')|\psi(0)\rangle}_{|\phi_n\rangle} - \underbrace{T_{n-1}(\hat{H}')|\psi(0)\rangle}_{|\phi_{n-1}\rangle}. \]
(59)

Hence

\[ |\phi_{n+1}\rangle = 2\hat{H}'|\phi_n\rangle - |\phi_{n-1}\rangle. \]
(60)

6.5 Step 5: Truncation and practical computation

In computation, we truncate the infinite series at some order \(N\):

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle \approx e^{-iat}\Big[ J_0(\tau)|\phi_0\rangle + 2\sum_{n=1}^{N} (-i)^n J_n(\tau)|\phi_n\rangle \Big]. \]
(61)

We generate \(|\phi_n\rangle\) for \(n=0,1,\dots,N\) using (60). Each iteration needs only one matrix–vector multiplication \(\hat{H}'|\phi_n\rangle\) plus vector additions.

7. Truncation Error and Why \(J_n(\tau)\) Gets Small for \(n\gtrsim\tau\)

7.1 Step 1: Define the remainder operator

Let

\[ \hat{U}'(\tau)\equiv e^{-i\tau\hat{H}'}. \]
(62)

Define the truncated approximation

\[ \hat{U}'_N(\tau)\equiv J_0(\tau)\hat{I} + 2\sum_{n=1}^{N}(-i)^n J_n(\tau)T_n(\hat{H}'). \]
(63)

Then the remainder is

\[ \hat{R}_N(\tau)\equiv \hat{U}'(\tau)-\hat{U}'_N(\tau)=2\sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} (-i)^n J_n(\tau)T_n(\hat{H}'). \]
(64)

7.2 Step 2: Prove \(\|T_n(\hat{H}')\|\le 1\)

In the eigenbasis of \(\hat{H}'\), the operator \(T_n(\hat{H}')\) has eigenvalues \(T_n(E_k')\). Since \(E_k'\in[-1,1]\), boundedness (10) gives \(|T_n(E_k')|\le 1\). Therefore the operator norm satisfies

\[ \|T_n(\hat{H}')\|\le 1. \]
(65)

7.3 Step 3: Derive the error bound

Take norms in (64) and use the triangle inequality:

\[ \|\hat{R}_N(\tau)\| \le 2\sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} |J_n(\tau)|\,\|T_n(\hat{H}')\|. \]
(66)
\[ \|\hat{R}_N(\tau)\| \le 2\sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} |J_n(\tau)|. \]
(67)

Thus

\[ \|\hat{U}'(\tau)-\hat{U}'_N(\tau)\| \le 2\sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} |J_n(\tau)|. \]
(68)

Applying to a normalized state \(\||\psi(0)\rangle\|=1\) gives

\[ \|\,|\psi_{\mathrm{exact}}(t)\rangle - |\psi_N(t)\rangle\,\| \le 2\sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} |J_n(\tau)|. \]
(69)

7.4 Step 4: Why the Bessel tail becomes small (detailed intuition)

Recall that \(J_n(\tau)\) appears as a Fourier coefficient of \(e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\):

\[ (-i)^n J_n(\tau)=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{0}^{\pi} e^{-i\tau\cos\theta}\cos(n\theta)\,d\theta. \]
(70)

For large \(n\), \(\cos(n\theta)\) oscillates rapidly as a function of \(\theta\), and the integral cancels due to alternating signs. More specifically, consider the oscillatory phase in the integrand:

\[ \Phi(\theta)=\tau\cos\theta - n\theta. \]
(71)

When \(n\gg\tau\), the term \(-n\theta\) dominates the \(\theta\)-variation, so \(\Phi(\theta)\) changes rapidly and cancellations are strong. This is the basic reason \(J_n(\tau)\) becomes small for \(n\gtrsim\tau\).

Hence a practical rule is:

\[ N\approx \tau + (\text{a modest safety margin}),\qquad \tau=bt. \]
(72)

8. Example 1: \(\hat{H}=\hat{\sigma}_x\) (No Rescaling Needed)

Let

\[ \hat{H}= \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1\\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad |\psi(0)\rangle= \begin{pmatrix} 1\\ 0 \end{pmatrix}. \]
(73)

Eigenvalues are \(\pm 1\), so \(E_{\min}=-1\), \(E_{\max}=1\). Then \(a=0\), \(b=1\), \(\hat{H}'=\hat{H}\), and \(\tau=t\).

8.1 Compute Chebyshev vectors explicitly

We have

\[ |\phi_0\rangle = |\psi(0)\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}. \]
(74)

Next,

\[ |\phi_1\rangle = \hat{H}'|\psi(0)\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}. \]
(75)

Now use the recurrence \(|\phi_{n+1}\rangle = 2\hat{H}'|\phi_n\rangle - |\phi_{n-1}\rangle\). Compute \(\hat{H}'|\phi_1\rangle\):

\[ \hat{H}'|\phi_1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}. \]

Then

\[ |\phi_2\rangle = 2\hat{H}'|\phi_1\rangle - |\phi_0\rangle = 2\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} = |\phi_0\rangle. \]
(76)

Similarly one finds \(|\phi_3\rangle=|\phi_1\rangle\), etc. Therefore

\[ |\phi_{2m}\rangle= \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad |\phi_{2m+1}\rangle= \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}. \]
(77)

8.2 Assemble the series (even/odd separation)

From (61) (with \(a=0\), \(\tau=t\)),

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle \approx J_0(t)|\phi_0\rangle + 2\sum_{n=1}^{N}(-i)^n J_n(t)|\phi_n\rangle. \]
(78)

Separate even and odd \(n\):

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle \approx \Big(J_0(t) + 2\sum_{m=1}^{\lfloor N/2\rfloor} (-i)^{2m}J_{2m}(t)\Big)|\phi_0\rangle + 2\sum_{m=0}^{\lfloor (N-1)/2\rfloor} (-i)^{2m+1}J_{2m+1}(t)|\phi_1\rangle. \]
(79)

Using \((-i)^{2m}=(-1)^m\) and \((-i)^{2m+1}=(-i)(-1)^m\):

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle \approx \Big(J_0(t) + 2\sum_{m\ge 1} (-1)^m J_{2m}(t)\Big)|\phi_0\rangle - i\Big(2\sum_{m\ge 0} (-1)^m J_{2m+1}(t)\Big)|\phi_1\rangle. \]
(80)

In the infinite limit these sums equal \(\cos t\) and \(\sin t\), giving the exact result

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \cos t\\ -i\sin t \end{pmatrix}. \]
(81)

9. Example 2: A Nontrivial Rescaling Example

Let

\[ \hat{H}= \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1\\ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad |\psi(0)\rangle= \begin{pmatrix} 1\\ 0 \end{pmatrix}. \]
(82)

9.1 Step 1: Compute eigenvalues exactly

Characteristic polynomial:

\[ \det\big(\hat{H}-\lambda\hat{I}\big)= \begin{vmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1\\ 1 & 4-\lambda \end{vmatrix} = (2-\lambda)(4-\lambda)-1 \]
(83)
\[ = \lambda^2 - 6\lambda + 7. \]
(84)

Solve \(\lambda^2-6\lambda+7=0\):

\[ \lambda_{\pm}= \frac{6\pm\sqrt{36-28}}{2} =\frac{6\pm\sqrt{8}}{2} = 3\pm\sqrt{2}. \]
(85)

Thus \(E_{\max}=3+\sqrt{2}\), \(E_{\min}=3-\sqrt{2}\).

9.2 Step 2: Compute \(a\), \(b\) and \(\hat{H}'\)

Using (16),

\[ a=\frac{E_{\max}+E_{\min}}{2}=3, \qquad b=\frac{E_{\max}-E_{\min}}{2}=\sqrt{2}. \]
(86)

Compute

\[ \hat{H}'=\frac{\hat{H}-a\hat{I}}{b} =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left( \begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&4\end{pmatrix} - 3\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix} \right) =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}-1&1\\1&1\end{pmatrix}. \]
(87)

Scaled time is \(\tau=bt=\sqrt{2}\,t\), and

\[ e^{-i\hat{H}t} = e^{-i3t}\,e^{-i\tau\hat{H}'}. \]
(88)

9.3 Step 3: Compute Chebyshev vectors explicitly

Initialize:

\[ |\phi_0\rangle = |\psi(0)\rangle= \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}. \]
(89)

Compute

\[ |\phi_1\rangle = \hat{H}'|\phi_0\rangle =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}-1&1\\1&1\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}-1\\1\end{pmatrix}. \]
(90)

Now compute \(\hat{H}'|\phi_1\rangle\) carefully:

\[ \hat{H}'|\phi_1\rangle =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}-1&1\\1&1\end{pmatrix} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}-1\\1\end{pmatrix}. \]
(91)
\[ =\frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} (-1)(-1)+1\cdot 1\\ 1\cdot(-1)+1\cdot 1 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix} =|\phi_0\rangle. \]
(92)

Then the recurrence gives

\[ |\phi_2\rangle = 2\hat{H}'|\phi_1\rangle - |\phi_0\rangle = 2|\phi_0\rangle - |\phi_0\rangle = |\phi_0\rangle. \]
(93)

Similarly, \(\hat{H}'|\phi_2\rangle=\hat{H}'|\phi_0\rangle=|\phi_1\rangle\Rightarrow |\phi_3\rangle=2\hat{H}'|\phi_2\rangle-|\phi_1\rangle=2|\phi_1\rangle-|\phi_1\rangle=|\phi_1\rangle\). So again we obtain the pattern

\[ |\phi_{2m}\rangle = |\phi_0\rangle, \qquad |\phi_{2m+1}\rangle = |\phi_1\rangle. \]
(94)

9.4 Step 4: Assemble the propagated state

Using (61),

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle \approx e^{-i3t} \Big[ J_0(\tau)|\phi_0\rangle + 2\sum_{n=1}^{N} (-i)^n J_n(\tau)|\phi_n\rangle \Big], \qquad \tau=\sqrt{2}\,t. \]
(95)

Separate even and odd \(n\) as before to get the explicit two-vector form:

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle \approx e^{-i3t}\big[ A_N(\tau)|\phi_0\rangle + B_N(\tau)|\phi_1\rangle \big], \]
(96)

where

\[ A_N(\tau)\equiv J_0(\tau) + 2\sum_{m=1}^{\lfloor N/2\rfloor} (-1)^m J_{2m}(\tau), \]
(97)
\[ B_N(\tau)\equiv -2i\sum_{m=0}^{\lfloor (N-1)/2\rfloor} (-1)^m J_{2m+1}(\tau). \]
(98)

In the limit \(N\to\infty\), \(A_N(\tau)\to\cos\tau\) and \(B_N(\tau)\to -i\sin\tau\), yielding

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = e^{-i3t}\Big[ \cos(\sqrt{2}\,t)|\phi_0\rangle - i\sin(\sqrt{2}\,t)|\phi_1\rangle \Big]. \]
(99)

10. Summary: The Complete Chebyshev Propagator

Given \(\hat{H}\) and \(|\psi(0)\rangle\):

  1. Choose bounds \(E_{\min},E_{\max}\) and compute \[ a=\frac{E_{\max}+E_{\min}}{2},\quad b=\frac{E_{\max}-E_{\min}}{2},\quad \hat{H}'=\frac{\hat{H}-a\hat{I}}{b},\quad \tau=bt. \]
  2. Generate Chebyshev vectors: \[ |\phi_0\rangle=|\psi(0)\rangle,\quad |\phi_1\rangle=\hat{H}'|\phi_0\rangle,\quad |\phi_{n+1}\rangle=2\hat{H}'|\phi_n\rangle-|\phi_{n-1}\rangle. \]
  3. Assemble: \[ |\psi(t)\rangle \approx e^{-iat}\Big[ J_0(\tau)|\phi_0\rangle + 2\sum_{n=1}^{N}(-i)^n J_n(\tau)|\phi_n\rangle \Big]. \]
  4. Choose \(N\) large enough that the tail \(2\sum_{n>N}|J_n(\tau)|\) is below your tolerance.
