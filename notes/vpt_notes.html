Variational polaron transform

These notes outline a variational polaron transformation for the Frenkel exciton model with site-local harmonic baths. The idea is to generalize the full polaron transform by introducing variational displacement parameters and determine them via a variational principle (e.g. Feynman–Bogoliubov). This allows one to interpolate between the bare exciton picture and the fully dressed polaron picture in a controlled way.

1. Frenkel exciton Hamiltonian with local baths

We consider an $N$-site Frenkel exciton model, where each site $n$ is coupled to its own local harmonic bath labeled by $(n,\nu)$:

\[ \hat{H} = \hat{H}_\mathrm{s} + \hat{H}_\mathrm{b} + \hat{H}_\mathrm{sb}. \]

The system Hamiltonian is

\[ \hat{H}_\mathrm{s} = \sum_n \varepsilon_n \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert + \sum_{n}\sum_{m\neq n} \Delta_{nm} \left( \lvert n \rangle \langle m \rvert + \lvert m \rangle \langle n \rvert \right), \]

where $\lvert n\rangle$ denotes the singly excited state localized on site $n$, $\varepsilon_n$ are site energies, and $\Delta_{nm}$ are electronic couplings.

The bath is a collection of independent harmonic oscillators,

\[ \hat{H}_\mathrm{b} = \sum_{n,\nu} \frac{1}{2} \left( \hat{P}_{n,\nu}^2 + \omega_{n,\nu}^2 \hat{R}_{n,\nu}^2 \right), \]

and the system–bath coupling is taken to be diagonal in the site basis:

\[ \hat{H}_\mathrm{sb} = - \sum_{n,\nu} g_{n,\nu}\, \hat{R}_{n,\nu}\, \lvert n\rangle \langle n\rvert. \]

It is convenient to write the full Hamiltonian so that the system energies already contain the reorganization shifts,

\[ \varepsilon_n = \varepsilon_n^\mathrm{bare} + \sum_{\nu} \frac{g_{n,\nu}^2}{2\omega_{n,\nu}^2}, \]

in analogy with the standard non-variational polaron transform.

2. Recap: full (non-variational) polaron transform

For later comparison, recall the full polaron transformation generated by

\[ \hat{S}_\mathrm{P} = i \sum_{n,\nu} \frac{g_{n,\nu}}{\omega_{n,\nu}^2} \hat{P}_{n,\nu} \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert. \]

With this choice, the transformed Hamiltonian

\[ \hat{H}_\mathrm{P} = e^{\hat{S}_\mathrm{P}} \hat{H}\, e^{-\hat{S}_\mathrm{P}} \]

has no linear system–bath coupling, at the cost of introducing bath-dependent dressing factors in the excitonic couplings:

\[ \hat{H}_\mathrm{P} = \sum_n \varepsilon_n \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert + \sum_{n}\sum_{m\neq n} \Delta_{nm} \left( e^{\hat{X}_{nm}}\lvert n\rangle\langle m\rvert + e^{-\hat{X}_{nm}}\lvert m\rangle\langle n\rvert \right) + \hat{H}_\mathrm{b}, \]

where

\[ \hat{X}_n = i \sum_\nu \frac{g_{n,\nu}}{\omega_{n,\nu}^2} \hat{P}_{n,\nu}, \qquad \hat{X}_{nm} = \hat{X}_n - \hat{X}_m. \]

This “full” polaron frame is exact, but may be suboptimal in regimes where the displacement is too large (e.g. weak coupling / high temperature), leading to strong dressing and possibly slow perturbative convergence in the transformed frame. This motivates a variational generalization.

3. Variational polaron transformation

In the variational polaron approach we replace the fixed displacements $g_{n,\nu}/\omega_{n,\nu}^2$ by variational parameters $f_{n,\nu}$:

\[ \hat{S} = i \sum_{n,\nu} f_{n,\nu}\, \hat{P}_{n,\nu}\, \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert. \]

The transformed Hamiltonian is

\[ \hat{H}_\mathrm{vPT} = e^{\hat{S}} \hat{H}\, e^{-\hat{S}}, \]

and we will later determine $\{f_{n,\nu}\}$ by minimizing an approximate free energy functional. The transformation rules for bath operators follow exactly as in the full polaron case, but with $f_{n,\nu}$ instead of $g_{n,\nu}/\omega_{n,\nu}^2$.

3.1 Transformation of bath operators

Using the Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff expansion, we obtain

\[ e^{\hat{S}} \hat{P}_{n,\nu} e^{-\hat{S}} = \hat{P}_{n,\nu}, \]

\[ e^{\hat{S}} \hat{R}_{n,\nu} e^{-\hat{S}} = \hat{R}_{n,\nu} + \left[\hat{S},\hat{R}_{n,\nu}\right] = \hat{R}_{n,\nu} + \sum_{k,\mu} i f_{k,\mu} [\hat{P}_{k,\mu}\lvert k\rangle\langle k\rvert,\hat{R}_{n,\nu}]. \]

Using $[\hat{P}_{k,\mu},\hat{R}_{n,\nu}] = -i\,\delta_{kn}\delta_{\mu\nu}$ and $[\lvert k\rangle\langle k\rvert,\hat{R}_{n,\nu}] = 0$, this gives

\[ e^{\hat{S}} \hat{R}_{n,\nu} e^{-\hat{S}} = \hat{R}_{n,\nu} + f_{n,\nu}\, \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert. \]

The projectors themselves are invariant:

\[ e^{\hat{S}} \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert e^{-\hat{S}} = \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert. \]

4. Transformed bath and system–bath Hamiltonian

4.1 Transform of $\hat{H}_\mathrm{b}$

We write

\[ \hat{H}_\mathrm{b} = \sum_{n,\nu} \frac{1}{2} \left( \hat{P}_{n,\nu}^2 + \omega_{n,\nu}^2 \hat{R}_{n,\nu}^2 \right). \]

Using the transformed operators,

\[ e^{\hat{S}} \hat{H}_\mathrm{b} e^{-\hat{S}} = \sum_{n,\nu} \frac{1}{2} \left( \hat{P}_{n,\nu}^2 + \omega_{n,\nu}^2 \big( \hat{R}_{n,\nu} + f_{n,\nu}\lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert \big)^2 \right). \]

Expanding the square,

\[ \big( \hat{R}_{n,\nu} + f_{n,\nu}\lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert \big)^2 = \hat{R}_{n,\nu}^2 + 2 f_{n,\nu}\hat{R}_{n,\nu}\lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert + f_{n,\nu}^2 \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert, \]

and noting that $\hat{R}_{n,\nu}$ commutes with $\lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert$, we obtain

\[ e^{\hat{S}} \hat{H}_\mathrm{b} e^{-\hat{S}} = \sum_{n,\nu} \frac{1}{2} \big( \hat{P}_{n,\nu}^2 + \omega_{n,\nu}^2 \hat{R}_{n,\nu}^2 \big) + \sum_{n,\nu} \omega_{n,\nu}^2 f_{n,\nu} \hat{R}_{n,\nu} \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert \]

\[ \quad + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{n,\nu} \omega_{n,\nu}^2 f_{n,\nu}^2 \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert. \]

4.2 Transform of $\hat{H}_\mathrm{sb}$

The system–bath coupling transforms as

\[ e^{\hat{S}} \hat{H}_\mathrm{sb} e^{-\hat{S}} = - \sum_{n,\nu} g_{n,\nu}\, e^{\hat{S}} \hat{R}_{n,\nu} e^{-\hat{S}}\, e^{\hat{S}} \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert e^{-\hat{S}}, \]

which, using the above results, yields

\[ e^{\hat{S}} \hat{H}_\mathrm{sb} e^{-\hat{S}} = - \sum_{n,\nu} g_{n,\nu} \big( \hat{R}_{n,\nu} + f_{n,\nu}\lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert \big) \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert. \]

Since $\hat{R}_{n,\nu}$ commutes with the projector, this becomes

\[ e^{\hat{S}} \hat{H}_\mathrm{sb} e^{-\hat{S}} = - \sum_{n,\nu} g_{n,\nu} \hat{R}_{n,\nu} \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert - \sum_{n,\nu} g_{n,\nu} f_{n,\nu} \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert. \]

4.3 Effective diagonal and residual coupling

Adding the contributions from $e^{\hat{S}}\hat{H}_\mathrm{b}e^{-\hat{S}}$ and $e^{\hat{S}}\hat{H}_\mathrm{sb}e^{-\hat{S}}$, the coefficient of $\hat{R}_{n,\nu}\lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert$ is

\[ \omega_{n,\nu}^2 f_{n,\nu} - g_{n,\nu}. \]

Thus, if we chose $f_{n,\nu} = g_{n,\nu}/\omega_{n,\nu}^2$, this term would vanish and we would recover the full polaron transform. In the variational case we allow residual linear couplings:

\[ \hat{H}_\mathrm{vPT} \supset \sum_{n,\nu} \big( \omega_{n,\nu}^2 f_{n,\nu} - g_{n,\nu} \big) \hat{R}_{n,\nu} \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert. \]

The diagonal projector terms combine as

\[ \frac{1}{2}\omega_{n,\nu}^2 f_{n,\nu}^2 - g_{n,\nu} f_{n,\nu}, \]

which can be absorbed into a renormalized site energy:

\[ \tilde{\varepsilon}_n = \varepsilon_n + \sum_{\nu} \left( \frac{1}{2}\omega_{n,\nu}^2 f_{n,\nu}^2 - g_{n,\nu} f_{n,\nu} \right). \]

5. Transformed off-diagonal excitonic couplings

For $n\neq m$, the operator $\lvert n\rangle\langle m\rvert$ transforms as

\[ e^{\hat{S}} \lvert n\rangle\langle m\rvert e^{-\hat{S}} = e^{\hat{X}_{nm}} \lvert n\rangle\langle m\rvert, \]

where we define the collective momentum operators

\[ \hat{X}_n = i \sum_\nu f_{n,\nu}\hat{P}_{n,\nu}, \qquad \hat{X}_{nm} = \hat{X}_n - \hat{X}_m. \]

The proof is identical to the full polaron case: the first commutator is

\[ [\hat{S},\lvert n\rangle\langle m\rvert] = (\hat{X}_n - \hat{X}_m)\lvert n\rangle\langle m\rvert, \]

and nested commutators generate powers of $(\hat{X}_n - \hat{X}_m)$, which resum to $e^{\hat{X}_{nm}}$.

Thus the transformed system Hamiltonian becomes

\[ e^{\hat{S}}\hat{H}_\mathrm{s}e^{-\hat{S}} = \sum_n \varepsilon_n \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert + \sum_{n}\sum_{m\neq n} \Delta_{nm} \left( e^{\hat{X}_{nm}} \lvert n\rangle\langle m\rvert + e^{-\hat{X}_{nm}} \lvert m\rangle\langle n\rvert \right). \]

Combining everything, the variationally transformed Hamiltonian can be written as

\[ \hat{H}_\mathrm{vPT} = \sum_n \tilde{\varepsilon}_n \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert + \sum_{n}\sum_{m\neq n} \Delta_{nm} \left( e^{\hat{X}_{nm}} \lvert n\rangle\langle m\rvert + e^{-\hat{X}_{nm}} \lvert m\rangle\langle n\rvert \right) \]

\[ \quad + \sum_{n,\nu} \frac{1}{2} \big( \hat{P}_{n,\nu}^2 + \omega_{n,\nu}^2 \hat{R}_{n,\nu}^2 \big) + \sum_{n,\nu} \big( \omega_{n,\nu}^2 f_{n,\nu} - g_{n,\nu} \big) \hat{R}_{n,\nu} \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert. \]

6. Variational determination of the displacement parameters

The set of parameters $\{f_{n,\nu}\}$ is chosen to optimize some variational criterion. A common choice is the Feynman–Bogoliubov upper bound on the free energy. We split the transformed Hamiltonian into

\[ \hat{H}_\mathrm{vPT} = \hat{H}_0 + \hat{H}_\mathrm{I}, \]

where $\hat{H}_0$ is a solvable reference Hamiltonian and $\hat{H}_\mathrm{I}$ is treated perturbatively. A convenient choice is to include in $\hat{H}_0$ the renormalized exciton energies and the bare bath,

\[ \hat{H}_0 = \sum_n \tilde{\varepsilon}_n \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert + \sum_{n,\nu} \frac{1}{2} \big( \hat{P}_{n,\nu}^2 + \omega_{n,\nu}^2 \hat{R}_{n,\nu}^2 \big), \]

while $\hat{H}_\mathrm{I}$ collects the bath-dressed off-diagonal couplings and the residual site-diagonal system–bath coupling,

\[ \hat{H}_\mathrm{I} = \sum_{n}\sum_{m\neq n} \Delta_{nm} \left( e^{\hat{X}_{nm}} \lvert n\rangle\langle m\rvert + e^{-\hat{X}_{nm}} \lvert m\rangle\langle n\rvert \right) + \sum_{n,\nu} \big( \omega_{n,\nu}^2 f_{n,\nu} - g_{n,\nu} \big) \hat{R}_{n,\nu} \lvert n\rangle\langle n\rvert. \]

The Feynman–Bogoliubov free energy functional at temperature $T = 1/\beta$ is

\[ F_\mathrm{FB} = F_0 + \langle \hat{H}_\mathrm{I} \rangle_0, \]

where

\[ F_0 = -\frac{1}{\beta} \ln \mathrm{Tr} \left[ e^{-\beta \hat{H}_0} \right], \qquad \langle \cdots \rangle_0 = \frac{ \mathrm{Tr} \left[ (\cdots)\, e^{-\beta \hat{H}_0} \right] }{ \mathrm{Tr} \left[ e^{-\beta \hat{H}_0} \right] }. \]

The optimal $\{f_{n,\nu}\}$ are obtained from the variational conditions

\[ \frac{\partial F_\mathrm{FB}}{\partial f_{n,\nu}} = 0 \quad \text{for all } (n,\nu). \]

Evaluating these derivatives requires the expectation values

  • $\langle e^{\pm \hat{X}_{nm}} \rangle_0$, which give the usual polaronic renormalization of the excitonic couplings;
  • $\langle \hat{R}_{n,\nu}\rangle_0 = 0$ (for harmonic baths at equilibrium);
  • $\langle \hat{X}_n^2 \rangle_0$, which are directly related to the bath spectral densities and temperature.

In particular, the renormalized couplings take the form

\[ \Delta_{nm}^\mathrm{eff} = \Delta_{nm} \, \langle e^{\hat{X}_{nm}} \rangle_0 = \Delta_{nm} \exp\big[ -\tfrac{1}{2} \langle \hat{X}_{nm}^2 \rangle_0 \big], \]

where

\[ \langle \hat{X}_{nm}^2 \rangle_0 = \sum_\nu f_{n,\nu}^2 \langle \hat{P}_{n,\nu}^2 \rangle_0 + \sum_\nu f_{m,\nu}^2 \langle \hat{P}_{m,\nu}^2 \rangle_0 - 2 \sum_\nu f_{n,\nu} f_{m,\nu} \langle \hat{P}_{n,\nu}\hat{P}_{m,\nu} \rangle_0, \]

which simplifies because different baths are independent.

The resulting self-consistency equations for $f_{n,\nu}$ balance the gain in diagonal energy (through $\tilde{\varepsilon}_n$) against the suppression of coherent couplings (through $\Delta_{nm}^\mathrm{eff}$) and any residual linear system–bath interaction. In limiting cases:

  • Weak system–bath coupling $\Rightarrow f_{n,\nu}\to 0$: we recover the bare exciton Hamiltonian.
  • Strong coupling / low temperature $\Rightarrow f_{n,\nu}\to g_{n,\nu}/\omega_{n,\nu}^2$: we recover the full polaron transform.

The variational polaron frame thus smoothly interpolates between these two regimes and often provides a more accurate starting point for perturbative or approximate treatments of the residual interactions.
